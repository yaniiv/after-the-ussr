At the time of the breakup of the Soviet Union, there were 53 ethnic homelands, 15 of which became successor states to the Soviet Union. In the 14 newly created sovereign states, Members of the Russian diaspora had lost their status as a privileged majority and had to decide whether to leave or assimilate.
0. At the same time, the gates to emigration were finally opened. The entire former soviet population now had the option to leave, and restart somewhere else entirely.
Previous to the collapse, getting explicit permission from the government to travel abroad was highly unlikely. Traveling without it was a punishable by time in prison or worse.

When looking at net immigration, it's clear that Ukraine contributed the vast majority of Russian immigrants.
But when looking at the net percentage of Russians that left per country, we see that in all but 2 nations, more Russians decided to stay than go.
Despite continued movement into Russia, its population has been declining by 0.5% annually after peaking at 148.7 million in 1992. Two factors contributed most to this.
7. First, Russias birth rates plummeted just as its mortality rates spiked. This was a nation navigating the complete collapse of an ideology.
Second, as soon as the Soviet Union collapsed, it triggered mass immigration to the west.
8. Net Migration to the far abroad between 1995 and 2002 consisted of 3 groups: Germans, Russians, and Jews traveling to Germany, Israel, and the United States.
9. In Germany, migration consisted of of soviet germans attracted by the generous resettlement package for the aussiedler (cultural germans) and strong german economy. The aussiedler were welcomed enthusiastically as fellow German compatriots who would be a benefit to the economy.
Between 1992 and 2000 Germany received 550,000 emigrants from Russia, 60% of the total amount emigrating to the three main destinations.[7]
10. Meanwhile, Israel was welcoming soviet jewish migrants with open arms. Jews were persecuted as a matter of policy by the soviet regime. When the Iron Curtain came down, The 1990s Post-Soviet aliyah began en mass.
Between 1989 and 2006, over 1.6 million Soviet Jews and their families moved to Israel, protected under the “Law of Return” which stated that  "every Jew has the right to come to this country as an oleh [immigrant]."
11. Overall, About 979,000, or 61%, migrated to Israel. Another 325,000 migrated to the United States, and 219,000 migrated to Germany.
12. Previous to 1989 the United States was receiving almost as many Soviet Jewish immigrants as Israel was.
The United States had a policy of treating Soviet Jewish Immigrants as refugees under the Jackson-Vanik amendment.
13. However, in 1989 Mikahel Gorbachev lifted restrictions on emigration, and the United States stopped treating Soviet Jews as refugees.
