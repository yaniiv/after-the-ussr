After The USSR

On Christams Day of 1991, at 7:32pm, the Soviet flag was lowered for the last time. Without moving an inch, 25.3 million Russians became part of a large diaspora. Where did they go?

At the time of the breakup of the Soviet Union, there were 53 ethnic homelands, 15 of which became successor states to the Soviet Union. In the 14 newly created sovereign states, Members of the Russian diaspora had lost their status as a privileged majority and had to decide whether to leave or assimilate.
At the same time, the gates to emigration were finally opened. The entire former soviet population now had the option to leave, and restart somewhere else entirely.
Previous to the collapse, getting explicit permission from the government to travel abroad was highly unlikely. Traveling without it was punishable by prison time or worse.
Emigration policies during the Soviet Era were so stricly limited, they averaged at only 3,000 per year in the mid-1980’s.
To get a snapshot of Post-Soviet migration patterns between 1989 and 2002, we’ll look at:
1. the reconcentration of ethnic Russians around their homeland
2. the primary streams of emigration to countries outside the former USSR

In 1989, Russia had a population of 147 million. Ukraine had about a third it's population, always had a history as the largest Russian Diaspora nation in the world.
During Joseph Stalin's rule (1928–1953), masses of people were exiled to Kazakstans infamous gulags. This included Russians accused of being “enemies of the people”.
Uzbekestian had gathered a large russian population during World War II when masses of industries and their employees were moved there from Ukraine to protect them from threat in the west.
When looking at net immigration, it's clear that Ukraine contributed the vast majority of Russian immigrants deciding to return home.
What’s interesting is that in spite of continued movement into the country, Russias population has been declining by 0.5% annually after peaking at 148.7 million in 1992.
Two factors contributed most to this. First, Russias birth rates plummeted just as its mortality rates spiked. This was a nation navigating the complete collapse of an ideology.
The other significant factor was emigration to other countries
Migration to the far abroad consisted of 3 groups:
Germans, Russians, and Jews traveling to
Germany, Israel and the United States.
In Germany, net migration consisted of soviet germans attracted by the generous resettlement package for the aussiedler (cultural germans) and strong german economy. The aussiedler were welcomed enthusiastically as fellow German compatriots who would be a benefit to the economy
Meanwhile, Israel was welcoming soviet jewish migrants with open arms. Jews were persecuted and oppressed as a matter of policy by the soviet regime. As soon as the Iron Curtain came down, The 1990s Post-Soviet aliyah began en mass. Between 1989 and 2006, over 1.6 million soviet jews and their non-jewish relatives and spouses moved to Israel, protected under the “Law of Return” which stated that  "every Jew has the right to come to this country as an oleh [immigrant]."
Overall, About 61%, of Soviet Jews migrated to Israel. Another 325,000 migrated to the United States, and 219,000 migrated to Germany.[2][3]
The United States was an important benefactor of all soviet immigrants. Between 1974 and 1990 the majority of Soviet Jews chose to travel to the United States over Israel. The United States had a policy of treating Soviet Jews as refugees under the Jackson-Vanik amendment
