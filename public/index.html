After The USSR

On Christams Day of 1991, at 7:32pm, the Soviet flag was lowered for the last time. Without moving an inch, 25.3 million Russians became part of a large diaspora. Where did they go?

At the time of the breakup of the Soviet Union, there were 53 ethnic homelands, 15 of which became successor states to the Soviet Union. In the 14 newly independent nation states, an average of 18.2% of the population consisted of Russians. Members of this Russian diaspora had lost their status as a privileged majority and had to decide wether to leave or assimilate.

At the same time, international emigration for the entire Soviet population became an option for the first time since 1922. Previous to the collapse, getting explicit permission from the government to travel abroad was highly unlikely. Traveling without it was punishable by prison time and in some cases death.

To get a snapshot of Post-Soviet migration patterns, we’ll look at the reconcentration of Russians around their homeland, and take a glimpse at some of the primary streams of migration to countries outside the former USSR between 1989 and 2007.

The same 3 countries had the largest number of diaspora members. Russians in Ukraine represented 45% of the entire diaspora.

When looking at net immigration, we see that also Ukraine contributed the most to Russias net population increase due to diaspora migration.

What’s interesting is that in spite of continued movement into the country, Russias population has been declining by 0.5% annually after peaking at 148.7 million in 1992. Some of the decline is due to the steep increase in mortality rate and decrease in birth rate in Russia. The other part is due to internattion emigration

In Germany, net migration consisted of soviet germans attracted by the generous resettlement package for the aussiedler (cultural germans) and strong german economy. The aussiedler were welcomed enthusiastically as fellow German compatriots who would be a benefit to the economy

Meanwhile, Israel was welcoming soviet jewish migrants with open arms. Jews were persecuted and oppressed as a matter of policy by the soviet regime. As soon as the Iron Curtain came down, The 1990s Post-Soviet aliyah began en mass. Between 1989 and 2006, over 1.6 million soviet jews and their non-jewish relatives and spouses moved to Israel, protected under the “Law of Return” which stated that  "every Jew has the right to come to this country as an oleh [immigrant]."

Overall, About 61%, of Soviet Jews migrated to Israel. Another 325,000 migrated to the United States, and 219,000 migrated to Germany.[2][3]

The United States was an important benefactor of all soviet immigrants. Between 1974 and 1990 the majority of Soviet Jews chose to travel to the United States over Israel. The United States had a policy of treating Soviet Jews as refugees under the Jackson-Vanik amendment
